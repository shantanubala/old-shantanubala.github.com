Security and Privacy

May 7th, 2013

Bruce Schneier’s Blog is an ecellent web site to read, especially if you are interested in issues related to security. Although a lot of his writing can be very technical, there are a few of his essays that make complex ideas easier to understand. One of my favorite posts from his blog is Security vs. Privacy, an analysis of the false dilemma that is often constructed between national security and individual privacy.

Not all security measures need to violate privacy, and not all violations of privacy lead to increased levels of security. An attempt to frame security and privacy as two opposing forces is misinformed at its best and pernicious at its worst.

To put it a step further, invasions of privacy are often attempts to profile people. By establishing patterns that describe a typical terrorist, we can narrow our searches and focus on people who actually exhibit behavior that resembles known terrorists. The standard argument is, “if you have nothing to hide, why should you care?”

Although privacy is important for its own sake, using personal information like Internet usage or private emails to find suspected terrorists is not a very good idea. A person with the desire to execute a complex terror plot will probably not send vital information over an unencrypted channel. Unless the NSA can outsmart GPG and TLS, the invasions of privacy will only foil terror plots conducted by unsophisticated groups. The benefits associated with sacrifices of privacy would be marginal at best. The end result will be an ocean of information available to government agencies about ordinary people without the knowledge to protect themselves.

This is the same reason why pilots have to go through airport security. Even though a pilot can crash the plane, the security concern is not about the potential damage a pilot can cause. The security concern is about whether the pilot is actually a pilot. When designing a security system, the security of the whole system is only as good as the weakest link. If pilots don’t have to go through ordinary security lines, a terrorist would only need a pilot’s uniform and badge to go through security undetected.

Likewise, if we use people’s private information, whether it’s emails or Facebook activity, we should be careful not to create a tiered security system. If a potential terrorist only needs to act normal on Facebook in order to avoid the government’s attention, monitoring Facebook accounts will not actually provide more security.

Security is always a matter of cost and benefit, but privacy is rarely an effective price to pay.
