What Startups Can Learn From the Cola Wars

May 27th, 2013

As I’m building my first company, I’m constantly searching for inspiration in a variety of places: interviews, biographies, and essays. Over time, I’ve found that analyzing rivalries between companies is the most interesting way to learn about strategy. It’s like watching two fighters in a ring to see who has better technique.

Why not look at one of the most famous consumer product rivalries in recent history? That’s right: Coke vs. Pepsi.

Split testing, not sip testing.

Nearly 8 years ago, Malcolm Gladwell wrote about the Pepsi Challenge in Blink. The Pepsi Challenge was a marketing campaign: instead of buying based on brand, PepsiCo “challenged” people to a blind taste test: take a sip of two colas and judge for yourself.

The verdict? People like Pepsi better than Coke. This caused a panic in Coke headquarters and resulted in one of the biggest flops of soft drink history: New Coke, an attempt to reformulate Coca Cola to make it more appealing in taste tests.

If Pepsi was better, why did Coke keep its market share? If New Coke was more appealing in taste tests, why did people hate it?

As Gladwell points out, the sip test was a flawed methodology. It didn’t test how much of the soda people consumed. There is a difference between drinking an entire can of soda and drinking a few milliliters. Nobody purchases a can of soda only to take a sip and throw the rest away, but that was exactly the variable they were attempting to optimize.

In startups, A/B testing has become very popular. There are tools like Optimizely, Visual Web Site Optimizer, and A/Bingo that make the process extremely easy. But if the product you are creating involves more than just a single “buy now” button, you have to be very careful. Although A/B testing has become easier, testing true retention and engagement is a much more lengthy process that can take months to understand.

Testing is a powerful tool, but you have to make sure you aren’t administering sip tests when you’re trying to sell bottles.

Do you actually need to reformulate?

Coke was extremely popular in the 1980’s before New Coke was released in 1985. Coca Cola was making a product that people wanted and enjoyed. Coca Cola’s biggest mistake was assuming that customers wanted a better-tasting soda (on average) rather than the soda that they were already buying.

Technology companies make this mistake all the time: they look at competitors and think, “why didn’t we make that?” Microsoft had this response to Apple with the Zune, and Google recently had this response to Facebook with Google+.

It’s obvious from the third person: what differentiates a product is how it differs from the competition! Instead of trying to make “a better iPod,” Microsoft should have tried leveraging its own advantages (for example, Microsoft was excelling at console gaming with the Xbox) to find opportunities that Apple was missing or intentionally ignoring. Google+ Circles are an attempt to differentiate Google+ from Facebook by having a more realistic representation of our social lives, but will that be enough? Only time will tell.

The important point is that New Coke didn’t seem like a bad idea. The science proved it was superior, and the decisions appeared to be strategic. The hard part of finding success is learning to recognize our own failures before they actually take shape.

New Coke was undoubtedly the result of a few brainstorming sessions revolving around the topic, “How do we beat Pepsi?” When attempting to solve such a problem, it is also important to brainstorm, “How could this solution fail?”

Consistency is underappreciated or overappreciated?

People dislike change. Moreso, people dislike change when they don’t see the need for it.

New Coke was an unnecessary change. A consumer can understand that Coca Cola can no longer include cocaine in their product due to DEA regulations, but a consumer will not sympathize with a “reformulation” unless the amount of value he or she gains exceeds the cost of switching to the new product.

Startups by definition have to move fast and constantly be iterating. Change is intrinsically part of the game: if you can’t change fast enough, you lose.

This presents an interesting situation: the average benefit of changes seems to scale linearly with the number of users, but the average cost of changes scales at a greater rate. In other words, there is a point where those two intersect. After that point, it becomes harder and harder to find changes that bring more value than they cost.

When Coca Cola came up with New Coke, they were already too far past the point of intersection.

Branding has always been part of our social realities.

A consumer research study on Coca Cola has examined the behavior of people who collect Coca Cola products. An in-depth look at people visiting “the annual Coca-Cola Collectors Convention, three Coca-Cola collector-only events, and several brand specific retail outlets, including the World of Coca-Cola in Atlanta, Georgia and the Fifth Avenue Coca-Cola Store in New York City” has shown that “it isnâ€™t just the product they like, they like what the company stands for: ‘the Coca Cola mystique’ as more than one collector deemed it.”

People expressed how Coca Cola is “tied to the American way of life.” The goal of any company should be to develop that level of loyalty. You cannot develop that just by making good products. You have to make people happy and allow them to feel like they are sharing culture.

This was, in part, why the Pepsi Challenge failed. Pepsi still doesn’t have a clear value statement. When I think of Pepsi, I don’t have other parts of my life that are embedded with the brand. When I think of Coca Cola, I think of iconic commercials with football stars and the beauty of everyday happiness.

When you’re making software, you don’t have to be as melodramatic.

Github is one of the most recent examples of a company that excels at creating culture. Everything down to the Github 404 page has a unique geekiness to it, but more importantly, they actively encourage people to become part of the community. By embracing open source software projects, they encouraged people to integrate Github into their lives in a way that very few companies are able to. Nowadays, “open source” often implies “fork it on Github.” When I am about to start hacking on a project, the first thought is, “let’s make a Github repo.”

There are multiple “perfect” flavors.

Though Coca Cola “won” the cola war, PepsiCo is still a multi-billion dollar company that is here to stay.

Look a little further and you’ll see many other smaller brands that are still alive and profitable. Even no-name generic soft drinks will be purchased in a grocery store.

In the continuum of people’s preferences for cola, there are multiple spikes of different sizes. The goal of a company is to capitalize on one of the spikes.

In this sense, a startup seems to be like an attempt to navigate such a space. Sometimes, you’re in uncharted territory. Other times, you’re in an area with other people with the hope that you can climb faster and higher.

I can’t wait to see how it goes.
