Practicing Memorizing

May 2nd, 2013

I’m working on my ability to remember information. Instead of using memory “tricks” like the method of loci or chunking, I’m working on simply using a “brute force” approach to storing information in long term memory using flash cards.

Although this isn’t the most efficient way to remember information, it’s the best way to remember information unintentionally.

The problem with the method of loci and chunking is simple: those techniques only provide you with access to information within the context of the technique. The method of loci makes memorizing information very easy, but you need to focus on visualizing a spatial location to access your memories. Chunking groups small pieces of information into larger parts, but what if you need an individual piece at a random time?

The problem with a lot of memory techniques is that they don’t address the main issue with memory: when is it useful to know the information? Chunking and the method of loci are useful for memorizing a speech or for situations when you know in advance that you will need a piece of information.

What if I need information, but I can’t predict when or how that need will arise?

The best way, in that situation, is to create as many associations with related things as possible. As a result, I’m giving a few different flash card tools a try. With these tools, I’m attempting to memorize maps of the world, vocabulary words, historical events, and animal species. To make the process as effective as possible, I’m working on creating a set of images from Flickr corresponding to each of the words, and the flash cards will have multiple “definitions” that can point to a single word.

So far, I’ve enjoyed using the following tools:

  • Anki - a downloadable tool that tracks the intervals between correct and incorrect recall to maximize the probability of successfully storing a flash card in long term memory
  • Quizlet - a web-based social tool with thousands of existing sets of flash cards that are ripe for memorizing and learning
  • Memrise - a newer and sleeker web-based tool with fewer flash cards available to study, but incorporates gamification and tracking of accuracy to maximize recall

Problems in Education

Apr 12th, 2013

The specific project I was evaluating had only gotten $800,000 out of the maximum $2m. Its strategy was to purchase the male students iPod Touches, the female students makeovers, manicures, and pedicures at a local beauty parlor, and all students were offered an additional iPod Touch or Makeover, respectively, if they passed the exam at the end of the current year. The grant proposal had specifically listed these actions as being the goal of the proposal. If the iPods and makeovers were purchased, that constituted success.

This was from a LessWrong article about the problems with education.

Although this article outlines the problems with the way funds are distributed to our schools through grants, it is a problem with human nature.

Once we create a solution to a problem, we judge the success of our endeavours by the success with which we execute our solution instead of judging based on the metrics that define the problem.

The short answer: measure everything, and evaluate results based on the evidence.

The Attraction of Fan Fiction

Apr 11th, 2013

I just finished re-reading all of Eliezer Yudkowsky’s Harry Potter fanfic - Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality. After reading it again, I have a new appreciation for what Eliezer is attempting.

Writing fan fiction based on a series as popular as Harry Potter allows Eliezer to construct a new world from existing parts. Instead of having to spend enormous amounts of time establishing the histories and personalities of his characters, he can focus only on the parts that are different from the original work. It is through these differences that Eliezer is able to explain seemingly-complex concepts involving Bayesian Rationality, quantum mechanics, cognitive biases, and psychology.

The references to science seem forced at times, but the underlying story is gripping in a way that is very different from J.K. Rowling’s writing. Eliezer has elevated the art of fan fiction to a different level - instead of just constructing a story that extends the original, he constructs a story that transcends it.

Give it a shot. It’s worth reading.

Apr 10th, 2013

I just finished re-reading Musashi by Eiji Yoshikawa. It is possibly my favorite book, and I will write a more comprehensive book review another time. For now, I just wanted to share a few quotes that I thought were golden.

If you are striving to improve yourself to a point of perfection, read Musashi. If you are interested in martial arts, read Musashi. If you enjoy reading books, read Musashi. It is a gripping story that is written with wit and wisdom.

“Ah, even I’m beginning to think priests are crazy. Everywhere they go, they meet no one but people rushing toward hell.”

I am not so distant from the gods and the universe. I can touch them with the three-foot sword I carry. But not so long as I feel there is a distinction between nature and humankind. Not so long as I remain distant from the realm of the true expert, the fully developed man.”

You seem to be under the misconception that if you perform one brave deed, that alone makes you a samurai. Well it doesn’t! you let that one act of loyalty convince you of your righteousness. The more convinced you became, the more harm you caused yourself and everyone else.

In Musashi’s own (non-fiction) Book of Five Rings, he says:

Moonwalking With Einstein

Apr 8th, 2013

Moonwalking with Einstein by Joshua Foer is an excellent book. Although you can watch his TED talk to get a quick summary of the book’s main ideas, I would highly recommend reading the book.

The book details his journey from being an ordinary journalist to a memory champion - he went from having average memory to being able to memorize thousand-digit numbers with ease. The most startling part of the entire book: anyone can perform the same tricks with an average memory.

The memory techniques definitely seem like tricks, but they are very effective, and date back to Cicero’s speeches in the Roman Senate.

Although the specific techniques, like using spatial memory to remember different details, are interesting, there is another profound aspect to the book that cannot be overstated: simply because something appears to be extraordinary does not mean you have to be extraordinary to do it. At first glance, the ability to memorize thousands of random digits seems like it would require genius, when it truly just requires focus and practice.

If there is one important lesson to gain from the book, it is that focus and deliberate practice can produce seemingly superhuman results.

Surely You’re Joking Mr. Feynman

Apr 7th, 2013

Surely You’re Joking Mr. Feynman by Richard Feynman is one of the best autobiographical books I have ever read. The brilliance of the book is its seeming lack of structure - Feynman simply jumps between different stories about his life, but the end result is fascinating. Richard Feynman is possibly one of the most interesting people to have lived, and this book is good evidence for that.

The electron is a theory we use; it is so useful in understanding the way nature works that we can almost call it real.

There are gems hidden in the book like the above quote. It seems like an ordinary statement, but hidden inside of it is something really profound: Feynman is able to put very complex ideas into simple words. He is describing the difference between the map and the territory without invoking age-old philosophical discussions. Instead, he uses a semicolon.

I wanted very much to learn to draw, for a reason that I kept to myself: I wanted to convey an emotion I have about the beauty of the world. It’s difficult to describe because it’s an emotion. It’s analogous to the feeling one has in religion that has to do with a god that controls everything in the whole universe: there’s a generality aspect that you feel when you think about how things that appear so different and behave so differently are all run “behind the scenes” by the same organization, the same physical laws. It’s an appreciation of the mathematical beauty of nature, of how she works inside; a realization that the phenomena we see result from the complexity of the inner workings between atoms; a feeling of how dramatic and wonderful it is. It’s a feeling of awe — of scientific awe — which I felt could be communicated through a drawing to someone who had also had this emotion. It could remind him, for a moment, of this feeling about the glories of the universe.

Behind Feynman’s life is a desire to discover the world and bottle it within his mind. It appears that the book isn’t just intended as a way to store his memories. Instead, he closes the novel with a speech he wrote encouraging students to find environments in which their curiosity can flourish.
