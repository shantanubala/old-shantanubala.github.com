Creativity Is Often Just About Showing Up

May 10th, 2013

Inspiration is frequently misunderstood. When I was a young writer I looked for it in all the wrong places. In my twenties, I lived with my wife in a studio apartment just off Washington Square. Somehow I decided that the best writing time for me was late at night–I guessed that was when the muses would be running wild and delivering intoxicating poetic secrets. Perhaps I got this impression from Thelonious Monk’s ‘Round about Midnight” which I played over and over–it was so hauntingly beautiful and sad. In those days, after a late heavy dinner with a couple of beers topped off by more than a few drags of weed, I took my yellow legal pad into the chilly unsightly stairwell across from my front door and got ready to write the great American novel. Ugh, wrong move, Waitz. I recall sitting in the stairwell waiting for inspiration to strike until I was dozing off or feeling too cold. Some evenings when my wife was off taking classes at N.Y.U., for inspiration I maxed out the hifi with Ornette Coleman or John Coltrane blasting pure madness solos while I tried to compose my delicate pages. Wrong. Wrong. All wrong, Waitzkin.

Now, many years later, when I’m working on a book I write everyday except Sunday, when I watch football or go to the country with my wife. This routine has settled deeply inside. It gives me confidence. I’ve learned that pages will come if I go to my quiet office and stick with my routine. Back in the younger days, the unsightly stairwell seemed cool, but not now. I could never do my best work after a heavy meal or with the music blasting. It would be a distraction–an energy robber.

That’s an excerpt from an interview between Tim Ferris and Fred Waitzkin with Waitzkin discussing inspiration.

Inspiration is often seen as a kind of “eureka moment” where great ideas suddenly materialize. We view inspiration as an instantaneous event. Inspiration either happens, or it doesn’t.

This couldn’t be further from the truth.

The examples are everywhere. If you look at Mark Zuckerberg, Facebook was not his only success. Facebook was a culmination of years of software development throughout his teens. He didn’t randomly realize the power of the Internet and then decide to build a social network. He spent years on the Internet before realizing its potential uses. He spent years programming before building something great.

If you dislike anecdotal evidence (for good reasons!), there is empirical evidence to support this claim. Conscientousness has positive effects on life outcomes according to a variety of different measurements. The desire for excellence and a willingness to do hard work is a key factor in determining success.

Effectively, our intuitive notions of inspiration may just be a byproduct of our inability to consciously trace the origins of our best ideas. Instead of attempting to replicate the actions that led us to good ideas, we are caught in trying to replicate the situations that led us to good ideas. This is reflected by the fundamental attribution error: when we see other people’s successes, we associate the results with a property of the people themselves, but when we see our own successes, we associate the results with the situation we are in.

The hardest part of creative work is starting and continuing the work. “Inspiration” is the easy part.
